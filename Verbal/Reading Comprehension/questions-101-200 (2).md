## Passage 11 — Jurisprudence

> The doctrine of precedent presents itself as a constraint on judges: like cases must be decided alike, and yesterday's ruling binds today's. The skeptics are right that precedent underdetermines outcomes and wrong to conclude it does no work. A judge who distinguishes must offer a reason the difference matters, and that demand for a principled distinction is itself a discipline, ruling out some moves and forcing others into the open. Precedent binds not by dictating results but by constraining the reasons that may be given for them.
