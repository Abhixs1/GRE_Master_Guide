## Passage 1 — History of Science

> When historians describe the seventeenth-century mathematization of nature as a triumph of reason over superstition, they flatter the present at the expense of the past. The natural philosophers who quantified motion and light did not abandon metaphysics; they relocated it. Newton's absolute space was a theological commitment before it was a physical one, and his contemporaries recognized as much. The tidy story in which measurement displaces meaning survives less because it is accurate than because it is useful to a discipline eager to claim the Scientific Revolution as its founding myth. What the record shows is not the expulsion of the sacred but its migration into the very concepts we now call secular.

❌ C. trace the origins of absolute space — no origin story is given  
❌ D. compare seventeenth- and twentieth-century physics — twentieth-century physics never appears  
✅ E. challenge a self-flattering historical narrative — the whole passage attacks the "tidy story" as myth

## Passage 7 — Neuroscience

> The seductive image of the brain as a collection of dedicated centers — one for language, one for faces, one for fear — has guided neuroscience for a century, and it is not wrong so much as coarse. A patch of cortex is less a specialist than a versatile joiner, meaning different things in different coalitions. This does not dissolve localization but relocates it: function inheres less in places than in patterns of coordination among them. The map was never useless; it was merely mistaken about what the territory is made of.
