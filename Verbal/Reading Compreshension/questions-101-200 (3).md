## Passage 11 — Jurisprudence

> The doctrine of precedent presents itself as a constraint on judges: like cases must be decided alike, and yesterday's ruling binds today's. Skeptics reply that this constraint is illusory, since no two cases are identical and a judge determined to distinguish an unwelcome precedent can always find a difference. The skeptics are right that precedent underdetermines outcomes and wrong to conclude it does no work. A judge who distinguishes must offer a reason the difference matters, and that demand for a principled distinction is itself a discipline, ruling out some moves and forcing others into the open. Precedent binds not by dictating results but by constraining the reasons that may be given for them.

## Passage 12 — Linguistics

> The search for linguistic universals has often assumed that what all languages share must be innate, wired into the species rather than learned. But universality has other sources. To find a pattern everywhere is therefore not yet to explain it, and the leap from "universal" to "innate" skips the very steps that would make the inference sound. The tempting conclusion — that introspection is worthless — overshoots. What the evidence impugns is not our access to our mental states but our theories about them; we feel accurately and explain badly.
