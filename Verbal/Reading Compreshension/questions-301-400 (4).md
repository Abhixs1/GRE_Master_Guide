## Passage 31 — Behavioral Economics

> Nudges — small changes in how choices are presented that steer people toward better decisions without coercion — have been embraced as a gentle alternative to regulation. Their appeal is real, and so is a difficulty their champions understate. A nudge works by exploiting the very biases that make people predictable, which means the same technique that enrolls someone in a pension can, in other hands, enroll them in a scam. The method is neutral about ends; it borrows its ethics entirely from the nudger's intentions. To celebrate nudging as such is to praise a tool while ignoring the hand that wields it, and tools, unlike principles, do not choose their masters.

## Passage 33 — Climate Science

> Attribution science — the effort to say how much a given heat wave or flood owes to human-caused warming — has advanced rapidly, but its findings are easily misread. To report that warming made an event "three times more likely" is not to say the event would not have happened otherwise; rare events occur in stable climates too. Nor is it to license the opposite error, the claim that no single event can ever be attributed. Probabilities shift without any event becoming certain or impossible, and the honest statement lies between the headlines that overclaim and the skeptics who deny that attribution means anything. The science is careful; its translation into public speech usually is not.
